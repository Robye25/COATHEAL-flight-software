PREFACE 
 
The REXUS/BEXUS programme is realised under a bilateral Agency 
Agreement between the German Aerospace Center (DLR) and the Swedish 
National Space Agency (SNSA). The Swedish share of the payload has been 
made available to students from other European countries through a 
collaboration with the European Space Agency (ESA). EuroLaunch, a 
cooperation between the Swedish Space Corporation (SSC) and the Mobile 
Rocket Base (MORABA) of DLR, is responsible for the campaign management 
and operations of the launch vehicles. Experts from DLR, SSC, ZARM and ESA 
provide technical support to the student teams throughout the project. REXUS 
and BEXUS are launched from SSC, Esrange Space Center in northern 
Sweden. 
COATHEAL (In-Flight Demonstration of Self-Healing Coatings) is a student 
experiment developed by a multidisciplinary team from the University 
Politehnica of Bucharest, Military Technical Academy “Ferdinand I”, and the 
University of Bucharest, Romania. The aim of the experiment is to investigate 
the feasibility of autonomous self-healing coatings systems under stratospheric  
flight conditions, with a focus on damage mitigation mechanisms relevant to 
aerospace structures. COATHEAL is scheduled to fly aboard BX38 as part of 
cycle 17 of the Swedish-German programme REXUS/BEXUS. 
This Student Experiment Documentation (SED) presents all relevant 
information available at the current project stage, including the scientific 
motivation, experiment objectives and requirements, the conceptual and 
preliminary design of the experimental hardware, planned verification and 
testing activities, and the project schedule. Supported technical analyses, 
interface definitions and reference materials are also provided. The document 
will be updated as the design matures towards the Critical Design Review.

ABSTRACT 
 
The COATHEAL experiment investigates the in-flight behavior of a self-healing 
polymer coating applied to an aluminum substrate representative of aerospace 
structural materials. The experiment addresses the challenge of mitigating 
micro-scale damage in environments where inspection and repair are not 
possible, such as high-altitude and space applications. 
The self-healing mechanism is based on a polymeric coating incorporating 
microencapsulated healing agents that are released upon microcrack formation 
and subsequently polymerize to bridge the damaged region. Prior to flight, the 
coated samples are subjected to controlled mechanical pre-conditioning in 
order to introduce a reproducible damage state representative of material 
fatigue. During the BEXUS stratospheric balloon flight, additional microcrack 
activation is expected to occur as a result of combined mechanical and thermal 
loading under near-space conditions. The experiment focuses on the qualitative 
assessment of crack activation and healing response under flight-relevant 
conditions. 
The primary objective of COATHEAL is to investigate the technical feasibility of 
autonomous healing response under flight-relevant conditions, primarily 
through post-flight inspection activities. The results of this experiment are 
expected to support future applications in spacecraft structures, high-altitude 
platforms, and long-duration space missions, where passive material resilience 
can significantly enhance system reliability.

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Scientific/Technical Background  
 
The development of advanced protective coatings represents an important 
pathway toward enhancing the longevity and reliability of aerospace structures 
exposed to increasingly demanding operational environments. Aircraft and 
spacecraft surfaces endure constant degradation from mechanical abrasion, 
thermal cycling, ultraviolet radiation, and chemical attack, leading to microcrack 
formation that can compromise structural integrity and reduce operational 
lifespan. Traditional passive coating systems offer limited remediation once 
damage 
occurs, 
necessitating 
costly 
ground-based 
inspection 
and 
maintenance cycles. 
Recent advances in materials science have demonstrated the viability of 
automatic self-healing polymers that can repair microscale damage without 
external intervention. These intelligent material systems typically incorporate 
either microencapsulated healing agents or intrinsic reversible chemical 
networks that activate upon crack propagation. While terrestrial validation has 
shown promising results under controlled laboratory conditions, the 
performance of such coatings in stratospheric environments remains largely 
uncharacterized. The BEXUS platform offers a unique opportunity to evaluate 
self-healing coating technologies under these representative flight conditions, 
providing data essential for technology maturation toward aerospace 
applications. 
The COATHEAL experiment builds upon established research in polymeric 
self-healing mechanisms, specifically focusing on the in-flight demonstration of 
a microencapsulation-based coating system. The scientific rationale centers on 
quantifying healing efficiency, kinetics, and repeatability in a realistic aerospace 
environment, thereby bridging the gap between ground-based qualification and 
eventual 
high-altitude 
deployment.

1.2 Mission Statement 
 
The COATHEAL mission aims to demonstrate and characterize the self-healing 
capability of an advanced protective coating system during a stratospheric 
balloon flight. By exposing coated specimens to the extreme environmental 
conditions encountered at altitudes up to 30 kilometers, the experiment will 
generate critical performance data on healing efficiency under simultaneous 
thermal, pressure, and radiation stressors. This flight demonstration represents 
a necessary technology readiness level advancement toward autonomous 
health management systems for future aerospace vehicles, potentially reducing 
maintenance requirements and extending operational lifespans. The mission 
will validate the survivability and functionality of the integrated experiment

1.3 Experiment Objectives 
 
The experiment shall be considered successful if these objectives are achieved. 
 
Primary objectives: 
EO-1: Generate controlled microcracks in self-healing polymer coatings 
through thermally induced stress during stratospheric flight. 
EO-2: Expose coated specimens to stratospheric environment 
EO-3: Acquire, store, and recover complete experimental dataset for post-flight 
analysis of coating behavior. 
 
The following objectives represent additional experiment goals. 
EO-4: Demonstrate feasibility of secondary heating cycle for repeated 
microcrack generation within single flight. 
EO-5: Correlate environmental parameters with coating degradation and 
healing response. 
 
Success criteria:  
- microcracks confirmed in >4 specimens post-flight; crack dimensions 
10-50 µm width, 50-150 µm depth verified by microscopy 
- all data (specimen temperature, ambient conditions, crack detection 
signals) recovered and readable  
- statistical analysis completed post-flight 
 
1.4 Experiment Concept 
 
COATHEAL investigates the autonomous healing capability of microcapsule-
based polymer coatings under stratospheric conditions. The experiment 
exposes coated aluminum specimens to near-space environment aboard the 
BEXUS balloon gondola, where low pressure, extreme temperatures, and 
thermal gradients act simultaneously on the material. This validation will help 
assess the suitability of self-healing coatings for aerospace applications.

The 
self-healing 
mechanism 
relies 
on 
microcapsules 
containing 
dicyclopentadiene monomer embedded in a polymer matrix, with a Grubbs-type 
ruthenium catalyst dispersed in the coating. When microcracks propagate 
through the coating, the capsules rupture and release the monomer into the 
crack. Contact with the catalyst initiates ring-opening metathesis polymerization 
(ROMP), forming a solid polymer that seals the crack. This reaction is self-
initiating and localized, requiring no external activation. 
To evaluate this mechanism in flight, COATHEAL will use nine heated 
aluminum specimens mounted on a baseplate. Controlled thermal stress is 
applied via resistive heaters to induce microcrack formation during the 
stratospheric phase of the flight. Temperature sensors monitor each specimen 
individually, while ambient pressure and temperature are recorded to correlate 
environmental conditions with crack generation. All data is stored onboard and 
transmitted to ground via the BEXUS E-Link interface.  
Post-flight, specimens are analyzed using optical and electron microscopy to 
verify crack dimensions, assess healing efficiency, and confirm polymerized 
healing material formation. Correlation of these observations with in-flight 
thermal data enables evaluation of coating performance under near-space 
conditions.

3 PROJECT PLANNING  
 
3.1 Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) 
Project planning and progress tracking are managed through ClickUp. Task 
interdependencies are mapped in this platform, with visual representations and 
dependency chains to be included in the appendix. 
The project activities are mapped to nine work packages. Each package covers 
a specific area of the project and is assigned to individual team members. 
Work Package 1: Project Management and Documentation covers scheduling, 
risk tracking, and quality checks. This runs from the beginning of the cycle to 
finish. 
Work Package 2: Science and Coating Development handles the chemistry 
side: literature review, mixing the coating, preparing samples, and testing them 
before flight. 
Work Package 3: Mechanical Design and Manufacturing includes the CAD 
drawings, stress calculations, machining, and assembly. This needs for the 
requirements in WP1 to be finished. 
Work Package 4: Electronics and Integration covers gathering components, 
soldering, and putting the system together. This package needs mechanical 
interface details from WP3. 
Work Package 5: Software Development writes the code for the experiment and 
ground station. This runs alongside WP4 but needs hardware specs to finish. 
Work Package 6: Testing and Verification runs functional checks, 
environmental tests and requirements verification. 
Work Package 7: Launch Campaign Preparation handles safety paperwork, 
procedures, flight booking and delivery of experiment to the launch site. This 
follows WP6. 
Work Package 8: Post-flight and Data Analysis recovers the experiment, 
extracts data, analyses results, and writes the final report. This starts after the 
launch. 
Work Package 9: Outreach and Dissemination manages the website, social 
media, and events. This runs throughout the project but needs milestones from 
other packages to generate content. 
The longest chain is WP 1 – 3 – 4 – 6 – 7. Mechanical design must finish by 
CDR in May to allow manufacturing in June and July. Electronics needs the 
mechanical interfaces first. Testing waits for both hardware packages to finish. 
WP2 runs partly in parallel but needs chemical confirmation by April to finish 
formulation before CDR. WP5 has some float but must deliver before 
environmental testing in August. 
The May to July period carries the most risk. Manufacturing, electronics 
integration, and coating work happen at the same time.

3.2 Schedule 
 
The project schedule follows the BEXUS programme milestones: CDR in May, 
IPR in July, and launch in October. Activities are grouped around these fixed 
points, with internal deadlines for individual tasks. 
The project started in October 2025 with documentation and early technical 
work. PDR preparation occupied November through January. Following PDR, 
the detailed design phase runs from March to May 2026. Mechanical CAD, 
numerical simulations, electronics architecture and software development 
happen here. Coating development should move from candidate selection to 
preliminary testing. The microcracking system should be tested. 
After CDR, hardware implementation should begin immediately. Manufacturing 
and procurement run June through July, however what can be bought earlier 
will be bought (such as PT100 sensors and aluminum profiles). Assembly and 
integration follow, leading into the verification campaign from June through 
August. Environmental testing and end-to-end validation must finish before 
EAR.

individual panels screwed on a structural frame made from aluminium profiles. 
The experiment is composed of 3 main systems. 
1. Electrical Subsystem Housing  
The box contains electrical components (detailed further in Chapter 4.5). To 
maintain thermal stability at high altitudes, the interior is lined with insulation 
inspired by the BX2829 SHADE project. The entire enclosure is shielded by five 
aluminium plates and a protective top mesh to ensure structural integrity while 
also ensuring UV exposure.  
2. The Experiment Socket  
To protect the samples during recovery phase, the experiment socket is 
mounted on top of the structural frame as a detachable unit. This modular 
design allows the payload to be separated from the main structure for safe 
transportation. To mitigate thermal transfer from the heated samples to the 
socket, garolite spacers are integrated as a thermal barrier. 
3. Mechanical Integration  
The complete assembly is mounted to the BEXUS gondola (E-link/Gondola 
interface) using two 30 x 30 mm extruded aluminium profiles fixed on top of the 
ESCARGO. More details about mechanical design can be found in Chapter 
4.4.

4.4 Mechanical Design 
4.4.1 General Structure Overview 
 
The mechanical structure of the experiment is based on a double-shell 
configuration, designed to ensure physical separation between the harsh 
environment and COATHEAL payload. The mechanical assembly is divided 
into three functional layers: 
1. External housing: Sheet metal aluminium panels protect the experiment 
from debris and contaminants. 
2. Internal structure: Strut profile structure that ensures assembly’s rigidity 
and mounting points for ESCARGO. 
3. Thermal buffer: Foam layer designed to thermally insulate the electronics 
from the outside temperatures.

The COATHEAL samples have an updated shape in order to facilitate the 
appearance of micro-cracks. The system relies on constrained differential 
thermal expansion and this can be achieved by controlling the temperature of 
the samples with regards to the temperature of the frame. Having a bigger 
difference results in higher stresses and thus higher chances of bigger micro-
cracks. The experiment aims for 𝛥𝑡≈100 °𝐶 as this was the simulated value.

The shape of the samples facilitate the generation of stresses through the use 
of a bigger distance between the applied force location and the surface, thus 
bending is used as the main damaging action. The diamond shape has been 
picked as it has the biggest stress concentrating effect we have simulated. 
Detailed simulations can be found in section 4.4.4 Stress calculation. A thicker

Material was also changed to 2017-T4 aluminium alloy as 3003-H18 would 
have been very hard to source. This alloy has an elongation at rupture of 22%, 
thus being ductile enough to ensure a brittle fracture will not appear suddenly 
but also it is not too ductile thus, the mechanical stress will not result in 
deflection but in surface micro-cracks. As aluminium 2017 is alloyed with 
copper, internal stress concentrators appear [1] and this could help the 
propagation of substrate cracks to the surface. The 2024-T3 alloy was also 
considered as it has a lower elongation at rupture than 2017-T4 but it could not 
be sourced in time for tests. Use of 2024-T3 alloy still remains under 
consideration, if tests demonstrate that 2017 alloy is too ductile, 2024 alloy will 
be utilized 
As micro-cracking is a statistical field, tests will be conducted in the following 
weeks. The material was sourced already and machining operations have 
started. The alloy acquired was produced as a cast variant, that could raise the 
chance of internal stresses thus being beneficial for the experiment. The alloy 
in this configuration could be considered isotropic, unlike in a rolled state.

4.5 Electronics Design 
 
4.5.1 Electronics System Overview 
 
The main processing unit in the electronics system is a Raspberry Pi 4 which 
is suitable for the ambient temperatures reached during the stratospheric flight. 
Hardware longevity and data integrity will be obtained by keeping the 
temperature at a constant 0° C floor. Aluminium plates will be controlled from -
30°C hibernation state during ascent to a +70°C for the healing phase. To stay 
within the recommended 1.4 A (40.32 W) constant draw limit, the system 
utilizes dynamic power allocation and PWM throttling. 
The Control Unit, centered on the Raspberry Pi 4, executes the flight software 
and manages the logic for all phases of the experiment. Control Unit runs a 
custom PID control loop that processes real-time data from the sensor array to 
determine the desired duty cycles for the heaters. Raspberry Pi 4 manages 
thermal regulation and data logging at high-frequency, storing information from 
12 different sensors. By monitoring pressure and altitude data, the controller 
autonomously triggers the activation of healing phases while it remains 
accessible via the E-link for emergency overrides. 
The Sensor Subsystem performs data acquisition which will be used for real-
time and post-flight self-healing process analysis. For temperature 
measurements, ten PT100 RTD sensors are used, nine mounted under every 
aluminium plate and one for monitoring ambient temperature.  These sensors 
are interfaced through MIKROE-2815 RTD amplifiers on the SPI bus. For 

===PAGE 60===
- 60 - 
 
BX38_COATHEAL_SED_v2-0_08Mar2026.docx 
 
 
ultraviolet radiation measurements, the system has a BPW 21 photodiode. 
Attitude and pressure are monitored using a BME280 digital barometric sensor. 
The Heating Subsystem provides the thermal energy required for the 
experiment by utilizing nine 10 W Kapton heaters, mounted under every 
aluminium plate and one 10 W Heater in the Electronics Box. These actuators 
are controlled via a 9-channel MOSFET driver module that interfaces directly 
with the Pi’s GPIO pins. 
This setup allows for fine-grained Pulse Width Modulation, critical requirement 
for achieving approximately 0.85°C/s heat ramp for activation of the 
experiment. The software logic ensures that the heaters are prioritized, capping 
the electronic heater whenever the plates require a maximum ramp-up to stay 
within the 1.4 A safety envelope. 
Power Subsystem manages the distribution of power and serves as the primary 
interface between the experiment and the 28.8 V DC supply from the BEXUS 
gondola. 
For protecting the 5.1 V electronics from the voltage heater lines and noisy 
switching signals, a Pololu D24V50F5 buck converter is used. This regulator 
steps down the raw voltage to a stable 5.1 V at 5 A, providing clean power to 
the Pi4. This architecture ensures that power fluctuations caused by high-
current heater pulses do not interfere with the stability of the control unit or 
sensor readings. 
The Communication Subsystem facilitates real-time telemetry and ground 
control via the BEXUS E-link system. An Ethernet connection provides the 
physical link between the Raspberry Pi 4 and the gondolas communication hub. 
During flight, the experiment transmits a continuous telemetry packets, 
including sensors measurements, heaters states and power, allowing the 
ground support team to monitor mission phases. The uplink capability also 
provides remote shell access via TCP, ensuring that the team can perform 
diagnostics or reset the system if the autonomous flight logic unforeseen 
atmospheric conditions. 
 
4.5.2 Electronics Architecture and Logic 
The electronics architecture separates low-power logic from high-power logic 
from high-power actuation to prevent noise from interfering with sensor 
readings. A processing unit coordinates all systems and manages data flow and 
thermal regulation. The experiment electronics are organized into a central logic 
that interfaces with power, sensing, and thermal control subsystems.

The following block diagram shows the overall system architecture, highlighting 
the separation between the 28.8V power rails and the 5.1V logic signals. 
As shown in Figure 4.5.1, the primary power line has two distinct purposes. 
Firstly, it feeds into a Buck Converter which steps down the voltage value to a 
stable 5.1 V to power the Raspberry Pi 4 and the sensors subsystem. Secondly, 
the 28.8V line is routed directly to the Heater Control Path. 
Heater Control Path 
The heating subsystem maintains the electronics box at a survival temperature 
and helps the plates to reach their 70°C target temperature for the healing 
process. This path uses a 9-Channel MOSFET Driver that functions as an array 
of high-speed electronic switches. MOSFETs are connected to the 28.8 V 
power rail and controlled by the Raspberry Pi 4 via Pulse Width Modulation 
(PWM) signals. 
PWM use allows the control software to rapidly toggle the MOSFETs on and 
off, effectively “throttling” the power delivered to the nine 10W Plate Heaters 
and the 10W Electronics Box Heater.  
The Raspberry Pi  continuously calculates optimal power percentage for each 
heater based on real-time feedback from the temperature sensors and ajusts 
the duty cycle of the PWM signals to maintain the desired thermal profile. 
Data Flow and Acquisition 
The data flow is a multi-stage process that sends environmental data to ground-
based stations for monitoring. The Sensor Susbsystem collects data across 
several digital buses. Data is processed by the Raspberry Pi.  
The I2C Bus is used to poll the BME280 digital barometric sensor and the ADC, 
which transforms the analog signal from the BPW 21 UV photodiode. 
The SPI Bus manages 10 MIKROE-2815 RTD amplifiers, which read the values 
of the electrical resistance from the 10 PT100 RTD sensors located on the 
plates and inside the chamber. 
When the Raspberry Pi 4 receives the data, the flight software manages it in 
two ways. Internally, the data is immediately timestamped and written on an 
Industrial MicroSD card to ensure a persistent, non-volatile record of the 
observed data during flight.  
Simultaneously, the Pi splits this data into telemetry frames and transmits them 
over Ethernet to the BEXUS E-Link.

4.7 Power System 
4.7.1 Power Supply Overview 
 
The BEXUS 28.8 V DC bus powers the experiment. With a total energy need 
of 223.9 Wh, our current analysis suggests that using two batteries on the 
gondola may be needed to provide enough energy and safely handle the peak 
current draw of 1.73 A. 
The electrical system uses a simple power distribution setup to safely manage 
control and sensor electronics. A DC-DC buck converter creates a stable 5V 
supply for the Raspberry Pi 4 and the Pi-EzConnect HAT. The sensor array, 
which includes PT100 probes connected through MIKROE-2815 RTD 
amplifiers, as well as BME280 and BPW21 sensors, runs 3.3 V logic signals 
from this supply. The low-power logic circuits include filters to cut down on 
electromagnetic noise and voltage changes during operation. 
The safety plan focuses on real-time software monitoring and several layers of 
hardware protection. To lower the risk of electrical failures, the system uses 
fast-acting fuses and reverse-polarity protection at the main power inlet. 
4.7.2 Power Budget Calculation 
 
The table presents the current estimation of power usage during flight, 
incorporating the continuous theoretical draw of the computing and sensor 
subsystems as well as the dynamically throttled Kapton heaters. Although these 
figures are subject to refinement during physical testing and integration, the 
preliminary budget demonstrates that a two-battery configuration can reliably 
support the calculated 223.9 Wh total load over the anticipated flight duration. 
 
4.7.3 Duty Cycles and Operational Phases 
 
The experiment uses nine 10-watt Kapton heaters. They are placed under the 
sample plates. The thermal control program manages the power budget as a 
shared resource and uses time-division multiplexing. Running all nine heaters 
at once would need about 100 watts, which is more than the system’s power 
limit and the 223.9 watt-hour battery capacity. The software makes sure that no 
more than four heaters are on at the same time, so the thermal subsystem 
never draws more than 40 watts (1.43 A), and the system's peak stays at 48.23 

===PAGE 70===
- 70 - 
 
BX38_COATHEAL_SED_v2-0_08Mar2026.docx 
 
 
watts (1.73 A). The other six heaters wait in a queue and do not draw any 
current. By quickly switching which heaters are active using Pulse Width 
Modulation (PWM), the system keeps the right average duty cycles for all 
temperature zones without going over the power limit. 
During ascent, the main goal is to keep the self-healing coating inactive by 
holding the aluminum plates at -30°C. The plates get short bursts of heat to 
stop the samples from reaching very low temperatures. The most important 
change happens during the activation phase, when the controller heats the 
plates from -30°C up to the +70°C healing target at about 0.85°C per second. 
At this point, the multiplexer uses its full 48.23 W system peak, focusing on the 
nine sample plates to reach the needed temperature quickly. To stay within the 
four-heater limit, the electronics heater is temporarily set aside. The thermal 
mass of the extruded polystyrene (XPS) insulated box helps keep the inside 
temperature steady during this fast heating period. 
When the experiment reaches the float phase in the stratosphere, most heat is 
lost through radiation instead of convection. The multiplexing queue becomes 
less demanding as the system keeps the plates at +70°C for the 90-minute 
healing period, making sure energy use stays within the two-battery limit. 
During descent and recovery, the balloon moves into denser air, which 
increases cooling by convection. The thermal targets then change to a -20°C 
floor for the plates to stop the newly healed polymer from getting brittle. The 
system remains running so it can log data during landing and while waiting for 
the recovery team.

4.10.1 State of the Art in Self-Healing Coatings 
Self-healing materials are synthetically-created or fluid artificially made 
substances which through chemical and physical reactions,  have the inert 
ability to repair the affected regions, without external interventions [2], [3].

Self-healing composites have been placed in a class of systems that could 
repair their structure either autonomously, extrinsic, or with external aid, 
intrinsic [3]. 
Extrinsic Self-Healing 
Significant research on autonomic self-healing polymers has focused on crack-
filling mechanisms. These healing agents could consist of liquid monomers, 
catalysts, thermoplastic polymers, film-forming materials or  solvent solutions 
[2]. 
The main advantage of this technique is that it does not require the damage 
surface to come back into contact and exactly restructure the compound, 
especially due the polymer rigidity [2]. 
Although, the mechanism could crack-fill, only small areas, this approach is 
more suitable for addressing a wider range of damaged sizes. This self-healing 
system is more effective when the loading of  healing agent is sufficient for filling 
the entire cracked surface [2]. 
One of the most researched system for self-healing functionality  involves the 
incorporation of microcapsules containing a liquid monomer to be the healing 
agent and a catalyst fixed into the polymer matrix [2], [3], or in additional 
microcapsules [3]. The microcapsules rupture at the propagation of the material 
composite, causing the liquid monomer flow into the crack surfaces and upon 
contact with the catalyst, the crack heals [2], [3]. The healing results from the 
polymerization of the healing agents brought into contact with the catalyst [2], 
[3].  
This capsule-based self-healing system has been thoroughly investigated as it 
presents a wide range of applications, including coating industries, adhesive 
industries, and paint industries.  
One system was done using polyurethane microcapsules containing 
hexamethylene diisocianate as a core material in polymeric composites, 
enhancing the corrosion restistance of coatings [3]. The synthesis includes  the 
reaction of a diol-diisocyanate prepolymer based on 1,4-butendiol and toluene-
1,4-diisocyanate or 4,4-diphenylmethane diisocyanate to prepare polyurethane 
PU microcapsule shell [3].

4.10.2 Selected Healing Chemistry 
 
Healing agent 
On the basis of a thorough literature analysis, dicyclopentadiene DCPD was 
selected as the optimal choice as a healing agent.  
It could be easily associated with 5-ethylidene-2-norborene known as ENB, due 
to their utilization in the ring-opening metathesis polymerization mechanism, as 
part of self-healing systems. 
Given this similarity, a comparison between poly-urea-formaldehyde(PUF)-
dicyclopentadiene 
(DCPD) 
and 
melamine-urea-formaldehyde 
(MUF)-5-
ethylidene-2-norbornene (ENB) systems was performed in order to determine 
the suitability of the DCPD monomer.  
ENB, a colorless liquid compound is known for its greater ring resistivity and a 
higher reactivity compared to DCPD. During the laboratory procedure, both 
DCPD and ENB microcapsules were cured at 60°C for 16 h and at 80 °C for 2 
h, respectively [4]. It was observed that the microcapsules were not damaged, 
due to thermal tests performed at 60, 80, and 120 °C for an interval of 1, 2, 3, 
and 4 h [4]. 
Initially, by thermal treatment at 80°C, PUF-DCPD microcapsules had an 
average weight loss percentage of 13.93%, compared to only 9.63% in the case 
of MUF-ENB microcapsules [4]. 
Secondly, due to testing thermal stability at 120°C there was a 57.49% drop in 
mass for UPF-DCPD microcapsules, compared to only 19.23% for MUF-ENB 
microcapsules [4]. 
Despite the comparative analysis indicating ENB a potentially more suitable 
alternative, DCPD demonstrated superior compatibility with Grubbs’ catalyst 
introduced [4]. To evaluate the healing efficiency, the  microcapsules, along 
with the catalyst were treated at 40°C for 48 h [4]. The results showed a healing 
efficiency of 81% for the PUF-DCPD specimens and 77% for MUF-ENB 
specimens, when compared to the reference samples. Additionally, 
thermogravimetric analysis showed thermal degradation of 73.6% for the PUF-
DCPD system and 74% for MUF-ENB [4]. 
Dicyclopentadiene is a colorless to yellowish liquid hydrocarbon from the 
pyrolysis of hydrocarbon fractions. It is insoluble in water, readily soluble in 
acetone, dichloromethane, ethyl acetate, n-hexane and toluene. The strongly 
flammable compound could be found either as a liquid, or as a crystalline solid, 
harmful if swallowed and toxic by inhalation, but non-corrosive.  

===PAGE 78===
- 78 - 
 
BX38_COATHEAL_SED_v2-0_08Mar2026.docx 
 
 
ACGIH, The American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists has 
attributed an eight-hour occupational exposure limit of 5 ppm, or 0.5 ppm for 
European exposure limits. DCPD could be transported by road, rail, and ship, 
being widely recommended to be stored in mild steel or stainless-steel tanks, 
under controlled conditions, to minimize the risk of electrostatic discharge [5]. 
Dicyclopentadiene is formed due to Diels-Alder addition reaction between two 
cyclopentadiene units, both representing a diene and o dienophile [6]. The 
process is reversible, the reaction allowing the spontaneous dimerization of 
cyclopentadiene to regenerate dicyclopentadiene in the course of several 
hours. At 170 °C, the monomer could be isolated by distillation of the 
dicyclopentadiene [6]. 
The dimer exists in two stereoisomers, exo- and endo-dicyclopentadiene. 
Both of them exhibit chirality, a geometric property in which  a molecule or ion 
cannot be superposed on its mirror image by any combination of rotation, 
translations and conformational changes. 
It has been reported in the literature that exo-DCPD has higher rates in 
reactions like ROMP than endo-DCPD, using the Grubbs’ catalyst [7]. Various 
issues drew attention to the low reactivity of endo-DCPD, such as unfavorable 
steric interactions due to its bent geometry and possible coordination between 
the ruthenium centre of the Grubbs’ catalyst and the double bond of the 
dicyclopentadiene [7]. 
Despite the fact that, endo-isomer is the major product of the process, 
approximately 73%-83%, the exo-isomer of DCPD is best aligned with the 
experimental requirements, due to its low viscosity at temperatures reaching -
50°C, high reactivity and short reaction time. Thermodynamically, the exo-
isomer is approximately 0.7 kcal/mol more stable than the endo-isomer. 
Catalyst 
Grubbs catalyst is a ruthenium based complex used as a catalyst for olefin 
metathesis. Multiple Grubbs’ catalyst are known in the literature, the most 
popular being Benzylidene-bis(tricyclohexylphosphino)-dichlororuthenium, or 
as its usual name Grubbs First-generation G1. In addition to this are Grubbs 
Second G2 and Third-generation G3 and, also, Hoveyda-Grubbs catalyst. 
First-generation Grubbs catalyst was chosen, due to its various  positive results 
in ROMP reaction. However, G2 and G3 are well-known for their higher 
activities compared to G1, in this particular context, G1 may react more rapidly 
and effectively in combination with DCPD for ROMP. Because of its smaller 
ligand, it contributes to a more productive reaction. 
 
Ring-Opening Metathesis Polymerization-ROMP

Ring-Opening Metathesis Polymerization is a process where olefinic polymers 
are by metathesis reaction. The centre of the reaction is a Grubbs catalyst that 
contains a metal carbene intermediate that reacts with the olefins to form 
different olefinic compounds. This catalyst could split the double bond of the 
alkene and put the parts together with other alkenes. The ring opens and 
produce a different catalyst and alkene. 
The mechanism contains four stages to obtain a four member metallacycle 
intermediate [2]. Due to the reaction of cycloalkene and a metathesis catalyst, 
in this case, the reaction of DCPD and Grubbs catalyst. 
The second step is the opening of the metallacycle that leads to achieving a 
new alkene and catalyst. Then, the catalyst end reacts with a different 
cycloalkene producing a new metallacycle. The process continues till it forms a 
polymer chain [2]. 
ROMP reaction is facilitated by the cycloalcadiene strain. DCPD, having two 
available groups: the double bond of the norborene ring and the double bond 
of the cyclopentene ring, could easily react and form the polymer chain, with 
good mechanical properties [2], [8]

4.10.4 Coating-Substrate interface 
 
Based on extensive review of the scientific literature, aluminum and its alloys 
are widely applied in a variety of industries due to their low density, high 
strength, high conductivity and moderate corrosion resistance [10]. 
Pure aluminium is highly resistant to corrosion, except in environments that 
present a high or low pH. It does not need a protection compared to other 
metals [10].  
These alloys are particularly known to be used in automobile and  aeronautic 
and space sectors. One of the most employed aluminium alloy is AA2024-T3, 
especially for structural application in the aeronautical industry owing to its low 
cost and mechanical properties.  
The corrosion protection system needs to be resistant to temperatures between 
-55 and 80°C and stable for a long period of time [10]. 
Considering that they are susceptible to corrosion, the alloys were subjected to 
several treatments such as chemical oxidation and anodic oxidation. In 
addition, an efficient solution for the protection of aerospace aluminium alloys, 
is using organic coatings, due to their lower cost. 
Protective layers such as epoxy coatings are used because of their toughness 
and, because it is a simple method of applying to metallic materials. The coating 
is commonly serves as a primer to inhibit corrosion and ensure the adhesion of 
the topcoat. This technique is adopted in the aerospace and military industry. 
Due to literature research, a strategy for creating the chemical bond between 
aluminium alloy and a polymer was proposed, including two methods: Laser 
Engraving(LE) and Resin Pre-Coating (RPC) [11]. It was concluded that the 
second technique was more efficient due to the resin’s low-viscosity, 
overcoming the capillary limitations of the adhesives [11]. LE is a high-precision 
method used to increase the effectiveness of the created bond [11]. 
The purpose of the article is to show how epoxy pins are the optimal choice for 
a complete adhesion to the aluminium alloy [11]. Through the structural micro-
anchors the epoxy resin is blocked on the aluminium oxide and ensures a facile 
coating fixation [11].

For the experimental  synthesis, Biphenol A epichlorohydrin was used as the 
epoxy resin, and JL-F30 as the laser machine. The aluminium alloys were 
analyzed using the laser engraving system at 24W [11].  
A 10 wt% epoxy resin was mixed with acetone, the solvent used, to ensure that 
low viscosity. After the evaporation of the solvent, the epoxy coating was 
generated and successfully combined with the laser-engraved pores [11]. 
Several information about the experimental results are that the bond thickness 
was about 0.76 mm after 12 hours at room temperature, followed by a complete 
cross-linking after 72 hours at 60 °C was obtained [11]. 
The epoxy pins were successfully formed and had an average diameter of 
176.2 microns [11]. 
Ultimately, researchers discovered that 0.3 mm is the optimal choice for the 
pins to be fixed [11]. 
The anodization is a pretreatment technique for aluminium alloys due to its 
simplicity. The aluminium alloy is immersed in an electrolyte with anodic 
polarization, resulting an anodic film composed of a thin barrier layer and an 
outer thick porous layer. 
A typical aerospace corrosion protection system is a multi-layer system 
consisting of a porous anodic oxide, a corrosion inhibited organic primer and 
an organic coating [10]. 
Anodizing is an electrochemical method in which the operating conditions are 
extremely important to the final properties, chemistry and morphology of the 
anodic oxide film. The conditions include the temperature of the electrolyte and 
the electrode, the electrolyte pH and chemical composition and the current 
density [10]. 
One of the most important function of the anodic layer is to strengthen the 
adhesion of the organic coating to the metallic substrate. Adhesion implies a 
stable bond between oxide and the polymer [10]. In order to this adhesion 
functionality, it is essential to be involved physical and chemical bonds. London, 
Keesom and Deebye interactions take place between any two molecules in 
contact and have the ability to generate a covalent bond or ionic bond between 
the oxide and the polymer [10]. 
Another example is acid-base interactions that form between a donor and an 
acceptor of electron pair. In this case the existence of  hydrogen bonds is 
considered to be the most important factor for the adhesion between aluminium 
oxide and polymeric resins, particularly epoxy resins [10]. The hydroxyl groups 
of the epoxy resin interact with the hydroxyl groups of the aluminium oxide and 
generate the hydrogen bond [10]. 
Anodizing does not represent an independent process, being part of a complex 
corrosion protection system which presents pretreatments to prepare the 
substrate surface for the anodizing stage [10].

One first step is the elimination of any metallurgical lubricants. Cleaners and 
low alkaline substances are introduced to protect the aluminium [10]. The 
second step is alkaline etching, which removes the deformed layer near the 
surface formed [10]. It usually involves immersion in aqueous sodium hydroxide 
solution. The effect of this technique are the chemical modification and smut 
formation which means that insoluble elements such as iron and silicon could 
need further treatment to achieve a clean substrate [10]. 
As well as needing pretreatments in order to function, anodizing also requires 
post-treatments like sealing. The purpose of it is to close the open pores of the 
anodic oxide to ensure corrosion resistance [10]. 
The chemical stability, physical interfacial bonding and the strength of the 
interactions at the interface are fundamental factors in the high adhesion 
performance and corrosion stability [10].

4.11.1 Sample Specifications  
The experiment will use 9 samples made from 2017-T4 alloy of the same 
dimensions. In order to develop predisposition to micro-crack development, the 
samples will be mechanically fatigued pre-flight thus lowering the overall 
yielding stress by a factor. Although a proper S-N fatigue curve has not been 
researched for 2017-T4 alloy, an article [11] found a way of analytically 
determining it and the tests concluded that the method could be reliably used. 
For ensuring catastrophic damage will not occur, each sample will be 
mechanically to a maximum of 70% of the low cycle fatigue life for a particular 
stress. Low cycle fatigue would take place in the range of 104 and 105 cycles. 
Additionally, an article [12] concluded that a brittle film on a ductile substrate 
will induce cracks in the substrate if the film is damaged. The article used a TiN 
film on a brass substrate and observed that when the film starts to crack, the 
ductile substrate will also form micro-cracks because of the shockwave 
produced by the cracking. This can help produce more micro-cracks at the 
surface level. This will also be implemented into our experiment preparation as 
the samples will have a hard anodizing operation performed. The Al2O3 oxide, 
also known as alumina, is brittle by nature and that will ensure micro-cracks 
have a higher chance of appearing. Furthermore, alumina has a CTE of 
6.4 µm/m-°C while the aluminium substrate has a CTE of 23.6 µm/m-°C. This 
difference will result microcracks in the alumina film before reaching the yielding 
stress of it.  Hard anodizing the samples with a layer between 20 – 50 µm would 
ensure the layer would have a mechanical role not just corrosion resistance 
properties. Anodizing the samples will also act as an electrical isolation for the 
semiconductor film. The anodizing operation is detailed in chapter 4.10.4. 
 
4.11.2 Surface Preparation Sequence 
 
The surface preparation sequence establishes the layered structure required 
for the self-healing event and crack detection mechanism. The process involves 
three main stages: controlled oxide formation, semiconductor film deposition, 
and coating application. 
The aluminum alloy substrate first undergoes controlled anodization to form an 
aluminum oxide layer. The oxide layer is significantly more rigid and more brittle 
than the underlying aluminum. During thermal stress, aluminum tends to 
expand and contract more than the oxide layer, generating internal stresses at 
the interface. If the thermal expansion mismatch becomes sufficiently large, 
micro-cracks initiate in the oxide layer. These cracks will propagate (assuming 
correct layer dimensions and thermal system calibration) into the polymer 
coating that contains the microcapsules. 
A semiconductor film, several tens to hundreds of nanometers thick, will be 
deposited over the anodized layer. This film serves as the sensing element for 
crack detection. A small voltage will be applied across the semiconductor layer 
and resistance will be measured periodically. The semiconductor film exhibits 
stable initial electrical resistance and when a microcrack initiates in the

anodized layer, it propagates through the semiconductor film toward the 
coating. If polymerization occurs in the coating, partial crack closure may result 
in measurable resistance changes. Preliminary trials for semiconductor 
deposition have begun at the INFLPR plasma laboratory; film thickness and 
deposition parameters remain under evaluation. The self-healing coating is 
applied over the semiconductor layer. 
 
4.11.3 Pre-flight Handling and Shipping 
 
Samples are stored in a controlled environment at room temperature (15-25 
°C). To avoid premature unintentional microcracking before the flight, the 
storage facility isolates the samples from mechanical shocks and high-
frequency vibrations. 
The experiment will be sent to Esrange Space Center packed in a Pelican 
Protector 1620 case. To ensure thermal stability, we use Vacuum Insulated 
Panels (VIPs) between the case walls, and a closed-cell PE foam insert with 
hard-shell 22°C PCM bricks to maintain a constant internal temperature. 
Sorbothane dampening pads envelope the experiment box, absorbing 
omnidirectional impacts and vibrations during transit. To protect against 
moisture and static, the equipment will be sealed in an electrostatic discharge 
(ESD) moisture barrier bag with silica gel packs.  
This setup is designed to shield the experiment from environmental conditions, 
vibration, and impact during transport and is kept at a 15-25°C climate.  
A specialized international freight courier (e.g., FedEx, TNT, DHL Express 
Dangerous Goods) will oversee the shipment. The outer package will be clearly 
marked with "SCIENTIFIC SAMPLES - FRAGILE" labels and will include all 
necessary Safety Data Sheets (SDS) for customs clearance. 
The coating will be applied to the samples in Romania at The Petru Poni 
Macromolecular Institute. Since the specimens will be flight-ready upon arrival, 
no chemicals will be handled or applied at the Esrange facilities.

4.12.2 Thermal Crack Generation Mechanism 
 
As detailed in the Mechanical Design and 4.11 chapters, the crack generation 
mechanism exploits thermal expansion mismatch between the aluminium 
substrate, anodized layer, and polymer coating. Controlled heating of the cold 
specimens induces tensile stress at the coating-substrate interface, promoting 
microcrack nucleation and propagation through the coating thickness.

4.12.3 Electrical Observation 
 
The electrical observation system monitors crack formation and propagation 
through continuous resistance measurements across the semiconductor film. 
Due to power budget constraints, only three of nine samples include this 
detection capability, one witness sample from each thermal group. 
A low-current, low-voltage signal will be applied across the semiconductor film. 
Crack propagation interrupts the electrical path, producing measurable 
resistance changes that indicate crack initiation and propagation. Multiple thin 
semicondcutor strips can be deposited and connected to separate resistance 
measurements channels to improve crack localization data.  
Each monitored sample connects to two terminals for resistance measurement. 
The data acquisition system records resistance values with timestamps for 
correlation with thermal cycling data and ground-based analysis. The software 
stores resistance readings at defined intervals alongside temperature data. 
INFLPR has agreed to deposit the nanometric semicondcutor film over the 
anodized surface. Preliminary deposition trials are underway with the plasma 
deposition machine to evaluate layer thickness, deposition time, and surface 
roughness parameters. Silver (Ag) was selected as the semicondcutor material 
as it is already available at the institute.

7 DATA ANALYSIS AND RESULTS 
7.1 Data Analysis Plan 
 
After data extraction, preprocessing, and processing, the observed 
environmental parameters (temperature, pressure, and altitude) will be 
correlated with optical response signals obtained from the single UV 
photodiode. This correlation of multiple measurements will enable the 
identification of damage initiation, microcrack propagation, and potential 
healing onset during specific phases of the flight. The collected data will be 
entered into a table and organized into categories. 
In the laboratory, multiple tests will be performed such as SEM (Scanning 
Electron Microscopy) imagining for examining microstructural healing and crack 
closure, XPS (X-Ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy) for identifying polymer fill 
and confirming catalyst signature and optical microscopy and profilometry for 
crack measurement and thickness change. 
X-Ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy will start after samples are handled with 
gloves to prevent contamination and no conductive coating is applied, surface 
is cleaned by bombardment with inert gas ions to remove loose particles, then 
samples are mounted on conductive holders using carbon tape and electrical 
grounding is ensured to minimize charging. Also, before analysis, ultra-high 
vacuum conditions are achieved. Initially, a wide energy scan (0-1200 eV) is 
done to identify all surface elements and to confirm the presence of expected 
elements. Secondly, high-resolution scanning focuses on C (Carbon) 1s 
(polymer backbone and functional groups), O (Oxygen) 1s (oxidation, 
crosslinking, healing reactions), and catalyst-specific peaks. Other parameters 
analyzed by XPS are confirmation of polymer infiltration into cracks, 
identification of catalyst residues or active species, chemical bonding states, 
and evidence of curing reactions. 
Scanning Electron Microscopy will be performed after the samples are cut to 
expose the region containing the crack, cleaned with ethanol or isopropanol to 
remove surface contaminants, dried under nitrogen flow to avoid residue 
deposition and coated with a thin conductive layer (5-10 nm of gold, platinum 
or carbon using sputter coating. During the initial scan, low magnification 
imaging to locate cracks and healed regions and identification of reference 
points for before and after healing comparison are being done. Lastly, high 
magnification imaging (with magnifications from 1000x to 50000x) is used to 
observe crack edges, identify polymer flow into cracks, detect bridging, filling or 
sealing of cracks. 
Optical microscopy is performed right after sample preparation, which includes 
light polishing to enhance contrast at crack edges and cleaning of surface dust 
using compressed air or ethanol wipe. The magnification range used for 
analysis of the surface is 50x-500x. Cracks are identified and imaged before 
healing (reference images are stored for quantitative comparison). Same 
regions are reimaged after healing treatment, and alignment is ensured using 
surface markers or sample edges. Observed parameters through this method 

===PAGE 112===
- 112 - 
 
BX38_COATHEAL_SED_v2-0_08Mar2026.docx 
 
 
are crack length and width, degree of visible crack closure, uniformity of healing 
along crack path and presence of residual voids of discoloration. 
Profilometry (surface profiling) is made after samples are mounted securely to 
avoid tilting, and an undamaged area is selected as a baseline reference. 
Through profilometry, a pre-healing scan is performed (crack depth and profile 
are recorded) and a post-healing scan (same scan path is repeated, and direct 
comparison is accomplished) is achieved. Moreover, crack depth reduction and 
thickness increase due to polymer fill and healing uniformity across scanned 
regions are analyzed by using the profilometry method. 
 
7.2 Launch Campaign 
 
The flight preparations can be found in Chapter 6. 
The launch campaign constitutes the final phase of experiment verification and 
qualification, encompassing mechanical and electrical integration, functional 
testing, and operational validation prior to flight at Esrange Space Center. 
Prior to launch, the experiment will undergo comprehensive on-site functional 
testing. These activities include power-on verification, validation of the data 
acquisition system, confirmation of sensor operability, and verification of 
onboard data storage redundancy. 
The resistive heating elements integrated into the aluminum panels will be 
tested during the campaign to confirm controlled thermal cycling capability 
under imposed power conditions. Thermal readiness of the electronics 
enclosure will be assessed by combined passive insulation inspection and 
active heater testing, ensuring that all components remain within their qualified 
operating temperature limits during the expected flight phases.  
Mechanical integration will include verification of structural integrity, fastening 
security, and vibration robustness to ensure survivability during ascent, float, 
and descent phases. Electrical integration will be performed using the standard 
BEXUS 28 V DC power supply and the E-link communication interface. 
The autonomous operational sequence, including startup logic, data logging, 
telemetry transmission, and safe shutdown behavior, will be verified prior to 
final gondola integration. 
The launch campaign will conclude with a post-flight assessment, documenting 
experiment performance, deviations from expected behavior, and operational 
lessons learned relevant to future missions. 
 
7.3 Results 
 
Based on results reported in the scientific literature for microcapsule-based self-
healing coatings exposed to low temperature and reduced pressure

environments, the COATHEAL experiment is expected to demonstrate a 
detectable healing response following induced damage. When the experiment 
reaches the stratosphere, during thermal cycling, mechanical cracking initiates 
capsule rupture and monomer release. At the same time, local temperature and 
catalyst activation are enhanced by UV exposure, which leads to faster ROMP 
curing and sealing of the damaged area. 
Post-flight analysis will focus on the qualitative assessment of the coating 
condition using optical microscopy and SEM. Indicators of successful healing 
include partial crack closure and evidence of polymerized material within the 
damaged region. 
The experiment aims to demonstrate the occurrence and feasibility of the self-
healing mechanism under flight-relevant conditions. Successful performance of 
the experiment will be defined by the observation of polymerized material 
bridging damaged regions and by the structural integrity of the coating. These 
success criteria will be defined as we advance in programme phases and will 
be assessed through comparative pre-flight and post-flight inspection of the 
samples.
